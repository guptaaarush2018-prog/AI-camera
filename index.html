Simplified model for illustration — a single junction, one lane per approach, no turns, discharging at one vehicle every 2 seconds after 2 seconds of start-up lost time. Both sides receive the same generated arrivals, so the only variable is the controller. It is not the SUMO study, and it is not a measurement from a street.

The problem

A sensor you have to dig up the road to install

Where lights do adapt, they usually sense traffic with inductive loops — wire coils cut into the road surface. Installing them means closing the street. They fail invisibly. And all they really know is that something metal is sitting on top of them.

No direction. No queue length. No way to tell a bicycle from a lorry. Usually no pedestrians at all. And you need one per lane, so a junction with three-lane approaches needs twelve of them buried in the tarmac.

What a camera on the pole gets you instead

One camera covers every lane of an approach at once. It sees direction, speed, queue length, vehicle type and people waiting to cross. It costs a fraction of trenching a road, mounts on hardware that is already there, and can be repositioned with a screwdriver.

How it works

Four jobs, thirty times a second

1

See

The AI chip finds every object in the frame and labels it — car, bus, bicycle, person. This part runs on the pole, not in a data centre.

2

Remember

It works out that the car in this frame is the same car as last frame. That is what turns pictures into counts that don't double-count, plus speed, direction and queue length.

3

Decide

Twelve vehicles queued eastbound, one northbound with nothing behind it. So it proposes giving east more green — and only proposes.

4

Tell the neighbour

One small message down the street: twelve vehicles, arriving in forty seconds. The next light has its green ready. String them together and you get a green wave.

What leaves the pole is numbers, not video — about 100 bytes a second. A thousand junctions generate less network traffic than one video stream.

Cameras and lanes

One camera per road. Lanes are drawn in software.

A single camera resolves three or four lanes without difficulty, so a standard crossroads needs four — one per approach, mounted high on the mast arm opposite, looking back at oncoming traffic.

Lanes become zones drawn on the image once, at installation, each tagged with the movement its road markings permit. A left-turn-only lane's queue is the left-turn demand — the paint already told us what those vehicles want to do.

Which means that when the council re-stripes the junction, we edit a config file instead of closing the street.

Eastbound approach. Each vehicle is assigned to a zone by where its wheels meet the road, not by the middle of its box — at an angle, the middle of a lorry sits over the next lane along.

Shared lanes are the hard case, and we don't claim to read a driver's mind. A vehicle waiting in a "straight ahead or left" lane blocks everyone behind it either way, so the queue measurement still holds — and the system learns the real turning split by watching what vehicles actually did, a survey traffic engineers normally commission by hand every few years.

Measuring demand

Don't count cars. Count seconds.

"Give green to whoever has the most vehicles" is wrong — ten lorries and ten hatchbacks are not the same demand. But measuring the queue in metres instead is also wrong, and more subtly: a queue's length is mostly the gaps drivers leave, and gaps are not demand.

Green time is measured in seconds, so demand should be too. Every vehicle converts into passenger car units — how much junction capacity it eats — and a lane clears at roughly one car every two seconds once it gets moving.

Counting vehicles hands the green to the first row and makes the second wait. Weighted, they are identical demand — which is the correct answer.

Who gets green

Accumulated waiting time. It grows on its own while an approach is ignored, so a side road with two vehicles waiting 90 seconds outranks a main road with ten that just arrived — no fairness rule required.

How long for

Green holds while that queue is still discharging and ends the moment it runs dry. Switching is not free — every change spends around six seconds on amber, all-red and getting moving again, so a controller that flips whenever the other side looks busier is slower than a fixed timer.

What overrules both

Queue length in metres. If a queue is about to reach back into the junction upstream, that approach gets green regardless — a blocked junction can't clear even on green, and the jam spreads backwards.

The constraint that outranks everything

The AI is an optimiser, not an authority

A traffic signal is safety-critical. A fault that shows green in two conflicting directions kills people — both drivers look up, both see green, and neither is looking for the other.

So the vision system never drives the signal hardware. It hands over a request, and a separate, deliberately simple safety layer decides whether that request is allowed. If it isn't, it's rejected. If the AI crashes entirely, the light falls back to its ordinary timer and traffic keeps flowing.

The intelligent part is allowed to be wrong.
The safety part is not allowed to be complicated.

Conflicting greens can't happen, three times over

It can't be asked for

The AI never addresses individual lights. It picks from pre-approved phases — combinations an engineer has already certified as safe together — and chooses how long. There is no vocabulary for an unsafe combination.

It's checked anyway

A conflict matrix — a plain table of which movements clash — is verified before anything reaches the hardware.

The cabinet refuses

Signal cabinets already contain an independent conflict monitor watching the actual voltage on the actual lamps. See two conflicting greens and it physically disconnects the controller. It isn't ours, and it doesn't trust us.

The safety layer also enforces minimum green, the amber and all-red clearance intervals, a maximum wait on every approach, and a pedestrian guarantee. Those last ones exist specifically to stop the optimiser getting what it wants: an AI told to maximise vehicle throughput will starve a quiet pedestrian crossing, because that genuinely is the optimum. Which is exactly why that rule lives somewhere the optimiser can't argue with it.

Privacy

There is no footage to leak

Frames enter the detector and are discarded in memory. Nothing is written to disk, no plates are read, no faces are matched. What leaves the pole is a handful of numbers: how many, how fast, which way.

This is an engineering constraint, not a policy promise. A device that architecturally cannot produce footage is a different object from one configured not to — and that is the difference between a camera a neighbourhood accepts and one they campaign to remove.

Status

What's real, and what isn't

Running Detection and tracking on real hardware — stable IDs, speed, direction, and counts that don't double-count an occluded vehicle.
Running Lane zones, per-lane queue length, PCU-weighted clearance time, arrival rate, and a turning split learned from what vehicles were observed to do.
In progress Calibration, so those measurements come out in metres and seconds rather than pixels — and a study against footage of a real junction, since so far everything has only been tested on synthetic input.
Designed Junction-to-junction coordination over MQTT, and the signal advisory layer. Architected and simulated, not deployed.
Not claimed Nothing here is mounted on a public pole or controls a live signal. Nothing is safety-certified. Detection at night and in bad weather is untested, and every efficiency figure is simulated.

Being specific about this is not a weakness in the pitch. It is what makes the rest of it believable.
